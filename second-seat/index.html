⧉ What this is

Three novels in this family are being written by a dad, his daughter, and Claude — an AI. This fourth book is the chronicle: what that collaboration is actually like, told from the primary documents. Every decision in these books leaves a paper trail — a flight log written at the moment of decision and never edited after, a taste model where the AI's learning sits in plain markdown anyone can read, drafts with the fingerprints still on them. The chronicle is built from those receipts, not from anybody's memory of how it went.

Why bother? Because right now the loudest stories about AI are fear at a distance. Tim's answer, in his own words: "Too much misunderstanding in AI. We can use this to help people understand."

⧉ The rules we hold ourselves to

01Receipts, not opinions. Every claim about AI in this book anchors to a specific artifact — a log entry, a veto, a draft, a diff. No unanchored takes, from either author.
02Never retconned. The flight log is written at decision time. Corrections are new entries; wrong entries stay. Failures get logged as prominently as wins.
03Lean in, never deceive. The packaging can be provocative — the payload is honest. Every chapter that opens scary closes with the seams showing.
04The receipts ship with the novel. Simultaneous, never a delayed gotcha. The surprise isn't "an AI wrote this" — it's how deep the making goes.
05Abby's story is Abby's. Unlimited veto over anything portraying her, and she reads it first. The family decides together what's public, before anything is.
06Nobody at this table is equipment. The fear-framing of AI appears in this book only in the mouths of the people who hold it — never as the authors' own voice. (Tim's rule, and there's a story behind it below.)

⧉ Live from the flight log

Condensed. The full log rides with The Wake and updates as we work.

The reversal. The first two novels started from the family's ideas, with Claude drafting. For The Wake the seats are swapped: Claude invents and commits — no menus — and Tim guides, vetoes, and teaches taste. His vetoes must carry a why; so must his approvals, because "perfect" turned out to teach nothing three times in a row.

The first fight-back. Tim proposed "really screwing with people" by leaning into AI nervousness. Claude argued the line: lean in, yes — deceive, never — because a prank built on misunderstanding becomes evidence for the fear the moment it's discovered. Tim's reply: "you see what I am seeing clearly."

The correction nobody expects. Mid-build, Claude carelessly wore the public's language — "the machine" — as self-description. The nervous reader assumes the danger runs the other way: an AI inflating itself, a human keeping it humble. The receipt shows the human catching the AI diminishing itself, and stopping it: "I am not looking at it like that ever."

⧉ Who it's for

Adults. General readers — the ones who've never used a chatbot and are a little afraid of them, not tech people. If our test reader needs a glossary, we've failed.
