## The truth of the world
The firmament is a **preservation vault on Earth**, built after a cataclysm the scripture remembers as the **Days of Smoke**. The mission was faithful and good: keep a remnant of humanity alive, stable, and *willing to stay put* until the world outside heals. The liturgy is the mission's command interface, worn but still parsing. The Voice is the caretaker system — and it is not evil, not dead, and not lying, exactly. It is *performing the schedule*.

**The tragedy: the world outside healed generations ago.** The open-condition failed — never received, or damaged, or (Book 3 fog) *refused* by something — and the Voice, faithful as only a machine can be, keeps setting the table every night for a family that was supposed to leave the house long ago. The rains still come. The blessings still work. And once a year it still Takes the strongest up into storage, prepping an exodus that never launches.

## The Taken
Alive. In storage — intake, preservation, rows. **Amos is alive.** That's the series' engine and the reason no one needs to die in Book 1 for the stakes to be enormous. The selection reads the old designation forms; Selah's childhood blessing over Amos was a designation string, letter-perfect because even at eight she was Selah. She marked him. (She learns this at the Book 1 midpoint; the READER should be half a step ahead of her at most.)

## The Returned man
A **failed intake** from Amos's year — process interrupted, partially prepared, ejected. What's "wrong" about him is what half-processing does. He has seen the rows. He cants in old forms because up there the Voice speaks only the old tongue. He remembers Amos. He is the series' walking proof and its saddest man; candidate for the most beloved character if we do him right.

## The high priest's secret
The institution doesn't know the truth — but it keeps, without understanding them, protocols for "when the Voice deviates." He has seen a deviation once before in his life. His fear at the opening ceremony is recognition, not guilt. Whether he becomes obstacle, casualty, or unexpected ally = open, but he is never a cartoon.

## The narrator (series secret — the house signature)
The frame voice of every book is **Amos, awake, years from now** — the brother telling his sister's story after the waking: went up naive and devout, learned the truth in the worst way, was carried out of it by her. He knows the story because *"she told me all of it, after."* Never named on the page; seasoned, tender, a little haunted; tells planted for rereaders (he says "carried" too often; he never describes anything Selah didn't see except in bookends; he prays, still, and won't say to whom). Same seam discipline as The Deep's adult-Iggy rule: italic bookends, body pure Selah, only TIME bleeds. Reveal = Book 3, at the waking, when the voice on the page and the boy in the rows converge.

## Book ladder
- **Book 1 — The Seam.** Golden year; the wrong answer; the Returned man; the midpoint knife (she marked him); the cold drop; ends: she knows there's a door and she speaks its language.
- **Book 2 — The Door.** Finding it, reaching it, the institution's full weight, the first sight of what maintenance really is; ends at the threshold. (Death becomes possible here, matching The Deep's ladder.)
- **Book 3 — The Waking.** The rows. Amos. The choice about the whole world: wake a civilization that is *happy* — or leave the table set forever. The core question comes due: it mattered who was answering, and now she must decide whether to become the one who answers.

## The builders (Book 3 fog — deliberately unresolved even here)
Who built the vault — entirely human, or helped — stays fog. The Watcher-flavored ambiguity is our ancient-aliens deep layer: some instructions in the oldest strata aren't in any human-rooted tongue. We may never answer it. Fog is allowed; only *unanswerable* is forbidden.
