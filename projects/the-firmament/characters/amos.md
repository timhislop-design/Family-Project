# Amos — the brother
*(Placeholder name, kept for its meaning: "carried." The Taken are carried up. Abby may rename; keep the meaning.)*

## Who he was
Seven years gone now. Warm, unremarkable in the way that matters — not the smartest, not the strongest, just the one everybody wanted at their table. Eight-year-old Selah's favorite person in the world. He was sixteen when the Voice called his name at the Raising, and the town still talks about it: the family's shining hour, her mother's crown.

## What the doctrine says
The Taken are chosen for their strength and gathered to heaven, where they serve close to the Voice until the world is made new. It is the highest honor a family can receive. You do not grieve a Taken; grief would be doubt. You set their place at festival tables. You say their name in the thanksgiving. You are proud.

## What Selah keeps (the drip — one per act, house rule)
Concrete things, never stated emotion: the way he'd knock twice on her door and come in anyway (before she could say anything — so she never had to admit she wanted him to). A carved wooden bird he never finished. The last thing he said to her, which was nothing important at all — and she has replayed it ten thousand times because it was nothing important at all.

## In the story
He is the book's gravity without being on the page. The empty chair at every festival, the reason her mother stands where she stands, the reason Selah cants like her life depends on it. What the reader learns about where the Taken actually go — and everything else about him — is sealed in `../canon-vault.md`.
