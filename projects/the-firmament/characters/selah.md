# Selah
*(Protagonist. Name = the untranslatable word of the Psalms — a girl named for an instruction nobody can read anymore. Tim-locked; Abby veto allowed.)*

## Who she is
Fifteen. A **canter** — the guild-trained kids who hold the *unabridged old forms* of the liturgy in memory, word-perfect, because the old words must never be lost even though nobody uses them anymore. She's the best of her generation and the whole town knows it. She is also the sister of a Taken — her brother Amos, chosen at the Raising seven years ago — which makes her family blessed and makes her a small local celebrity twice over. She'd give anything to be neither.

## The pattern (house rule — the Nerai pattern, applied)
Anxious first, rises anyway. Dread and self-doubt run constant commentary; when the moment actually comes she steps forward and comes out clear — and it surprises her every time. **Her one island of confidence is the liturgy.** Inside the old forms she is sure, precise, unhurried, home. Everywhere else — hallways, market days, other girls' easy laughter — she feels like a fraud wearing a borrowed face. The town sees grace; she experiences stage fright that never fully leaves her body.

## The gift (no magic — house rule)
Perfect memory for the old forms, and the canter's ear that comes with it: she notices *variants*. Where a worn everyday blessing says one thing and the unabridged original says something stranger, she hears the difference the way other people hear a wrong note. Her whole power in this book is **attention**. Everything she discovers, she earns by noticing — nothing is given by blood, prophecy, or chosenness. (The old readings are always the stranger ones. The strange words are the ones that still do things.)

## The secret (her wound — spoiler-light version; deeper truth sealed in ../canon-vault.md)
The night before Amos's Raising, an eight-year-old Selah — proud of herself, showing off for God — sang the oldest blessing she knew over her sleeping brother. He was chosen at dawn. Everyone said the family was blessed. She has spent seven years secretly wondering whether she called it down, and has never said it out loud, not once. Her ferocious devotion — the extra hours, the perfection, being *good* — is atonement she's never named. She doesn't serve because she's sure. She serves because she's afraid of what it means if she stops.

## Look (canon so far — Abby's pass welcome)
Small, neat, contained: hair braided tight the way the old forms are braided tight, nothing out of place because everything inside her is. Quick eyes that notice before her face admits it. The kind of girl adults call "composed" without ever asking what the composure costs.
