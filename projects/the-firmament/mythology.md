# THE FIRMAMENT — The Real Anchors
*(The iceberg under the book: the actual mythology, scripture, and ancient-aliens material we're building on. Safe for Abby — no series spoilers here. Almost never on the page directly; it's load-bearing, not decoration.)*

## The cosmology we're taking literally
Ancient Hebrew cosmology, which the Bible's oldest layers describe plainly and which everyone politely reads as metaphor:
- **The raqia (רָקִיעַ)** — the *firmament*, a solid vault or beaten-out dome over the earth (Genesis 1:6–8). Root meaning: something hammered flat, like metal. In our world: it's exactly that.
- **The waters above** — Genesis puts an ocean *above* the firmament. Our world: true. You can hear them in a certain kind of storm.
- **The windows of heaven** — Genesis 7:11, Malachi 3:10: rain falls when the windows open. Our world: literally the irrigation schedule. The harvest blessing is the request string.
- **The lights** — Genesis 1:14 calls sun, moon, stars *lights set in the vault*. Not worlds. Fixtures. Ours are.
- **Wandering stars** — Jude 1:13's eerie phrase (Greek: *planetes*, wanderers). In our world the wandering stars are the angels — everyone can watch them move against the fixed lights, which is why no one in this world has ever doubted. Maintenance has always been visible.

## The language layer
- **"Amen"** — from the Hebrew root for *firm, confirmed, so be it*. In our world: the execute command at the end of an instruction string. Every prayer in the land ends by pressing enter, and no one knows.
- **Liturgy as preserved syntax** — real-world liturgical languages (Latin mass, Old Church Slavonic, Vedic Sanskrit, Quranic Arabic) preserve dead grammar for centuries with astonishing fidelity, because ritual repetition is a preservation technology. That's the load-bearing realism of our premise: a command language COULD survive two thousand years — but only in the mouths of specialists, and worn smoother every generation. The canters are that technology made into a guild.
- **Selah (סֶלָה)** — appears 74 times in the Psalms; nobody alive knows what it means. Probably a musical or liturgical notation — a pause, a lift, an instruction to the performers rather than a word of the song. A girl named for an *instruction nobody can read anymore* — that's the whole book in a name.
- **Textual criticism** — the real scholarly discipline of comparing manuscript variants to reconstruct the oldest reading. Selah does textual criticism with her own memory as the manuscript. Her discoveries follow its real logic: the older reading is usually the *stranger* one (lectio difficilior). The strange words are the ones that still do things.

## The ancient-aliens thesis, played straight
The pop "ancient aliens" claim is that scripture and myth are garbled eyewitness reports of technology — chariots of fire, pillars of cloud, Ezekiel's wheels. We are not winking at this; we're building a world where it's the sober truth *of that world's own scripture*. Their Genesis remembers the Days of Smoke (their founding cataclysm — deliberately unexplained at this layer). Their Exodus remembers an intake. Their psalms are maintenance songs. The book's discipline: every "miracle" on the page must have one foot in a real scriptural image and one foot in a mechanism we could diagram but never do on the page.

## What the reader's own myths are doing here
For adult readers, every device in the world should rhyme with something real: manna (scheduled provision), the pillar of fire (guidance system), the ark of the covenant as dangerous hardware only one bloodline may touch, the tabernacle as an interface tent, Babel as a permissions change (parked — see ideas-parking-lot), Jacob's ladder as cargo path, the still small voice. We never say any of this. Rereaders and adults get the shiver; Abby's generation gets a world that simply works.

## Naming register (for Abby's naming passes)
Names in this world should feel liturgical-but-worn: real Hebrew/biblical roots sanded by two thousand years of drift, the way "Yohanan" became "John." Placeholders so far: Selah, Amos ("carried" — chosen because the Taken are carried up; keep the meaning even if the name changes). The rule from The Deep applies: say-it pronunciations and a name-why for every keeper.
