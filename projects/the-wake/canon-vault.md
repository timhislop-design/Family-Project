## What passed through

Not a ship. Not one thing. **A migration.** Vast, slow entities — working name **the Herd**, never named on-page in Book 1 — crossed the young solar system some four billion years ago, moving at grazing pace across a span of millions of years. They were not visiting. We were not here. The system was terrain.

The scars we call nature: the impact that made our oversized Moon, the bombardment record, the belt that never became a planet, the rings, the tilt of things. Real astronomy's genuine oddities, reread as spray. (House pattern from The Deep: the mythology is load-bearing and checkable — here the "mythology" is the actual published weirdness of solar system formation.)

## What the wake is

Their passage left a **standing distortion in the grain of local spacetime** — not damage, a wake: still slowly relaxing after four billion years, the way a harbor keeps rocking after the ship is gone. The relaxation isn't uniform. **Crests** drift through the outer system on decade scales. When a crest crosses the line between two beacons, signal timing wobbles measurably — which is why, once a decade, a light answers half a second **early**. Gran's fish-hooks are crest transits.

## Why the keepers are the only instrument (the bureaucratic accident)

Automation replaces its own hardware every upgrade cycle — every baseline resets, every calibration is new, every anomaly is written off as pre-upgrade noise. The keeper law — same families, same lights, same handwritten log format, six generations — accidentally built the only **century-baseline, same-instrument detector in human history**. The Hallorans are a telescope and don't know it. Destroy the archives, and the wake becomes undetectable for another hundred years. This is the engine of the whole series: the thing being thrown away as sentimental is the only thing that can see the truth.

## What the line points at

The relaxation gradient points **along the migration route**. Book 1's endpoint: the line terminates at a coordinate in the deep Kuiper where the gradient *doesn't* relax. Something is still there. **A straggler.** Not dead, not awake, not aware of us — a thing at the wrong timescale, too slow to notice a species that lives and dies inside one of its moments. It does not chase. It does not need to. That's the dread: it isn't coming for us; it just never left.

## The ladder (series spine)

- **Book 1 — THE WAKE:** prove the line, save the logs, keep the light. Ends: endpoint confirmed, archive rescued by an act of witness, the Halloran Light kept burning past its decommission date. No on-page deaths (Gran died before page one; she's everywhere anyway).
- **Book 2 — THE STRAGGLER (working):** what's at the coordinate; what humans — companies, agencies, the other keeper families — do once the logs are suddenly the most valuable objects in the system. The humans are the weather; the straggler just *is*.
- **Book 3 — THE FLYWAY (working):** the route is used. The migration is periodic on deep time, and the solar system sits on it. Series close = the theme at full scale: knowing something immense passes this way, does humanity extinguish the lights and hide — or keep them lit? The keepers' answer has been in the logbooks for a hundred and forty years. You keep the light. You write it down. That's what we're for.

## Hard rules

- **The wake is physical.** Every layer, every book — measurable, physical, strange but never supernatural. (Same spine as Firmament's no-non-physical-layer rule.)
- **Nothing chases anyone.** Ever. Dread comes from age, scale, and indifference — never pursuit. If a draft needs a chase to work, the draft is wrong.
- **The Herd is never explained.** Origin, purpose, destination — Book 3 may reveal the route, never the reason. Meaning belongs to the witnesses, not the witnessed.
