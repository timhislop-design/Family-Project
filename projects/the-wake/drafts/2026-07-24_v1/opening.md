# The Wake — Chapter One, opening (v1, first clay)

Close POV: Eli Halloran, 16, Halloran Station ("the Halloran Light"). House rules apply: journal test, drip don't dump, no cosmology named. This is clay — Tim edits hard, that's the process.

The scanner spat Gran's logbook out for the third time, and Eli was pretty sure it was doing it on purpose.

DISPOSAL MANIFEST — CLASS C: SENTIMENTAL / NO EVIDENTIARY VALUE. That was the sticker the company shipped out for the family archive. An actual roll of stickers. Like the Hallorans might try to smuggle a hundred and forty years of paper past somebody.

He flattened the warped cover with his elbow and fed it through again. Every Halloran on this light had written the log every shift since before there was a company — pressure, traffic, drift, and what Gran called weather, which out here meant nothing and she recorded it anyway. The handover order said the archive should be digitized at best effort, low priority. Which was corporate for: no one will ever read this.

Fair enough. No one read it while they were writing it, either. That was sort of the whole point of a log.

Page four-hundred-and-something, Gran's handwriting going uphill the way it did when she got excited: answered 1401.997, sent 1402.5. And in the margin, her little fish-hook mark. Half a second early. A beacon can't answer half a second before you ping it, which is why nobody ever had to explain it, the same way nobody explains a creaky door.

He'd seen the fish-hook before. He flipped back. He'd seen it a lot.

The joke was supposed to be for his sister: plot every fish-hook on one chart, sixty years of them, prove Gran had been slowly losing it the way Dad always half-said she was. Ha ha. Look at the crazy scatter.

The line came out straight.

And the last point on it was three weeks old, in Dad's handwriting.
