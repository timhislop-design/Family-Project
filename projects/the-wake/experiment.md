# The Experiment — Rules of the Reversed Seats

**Agreed:** July 24, 2026. This file is the protocol. If we change a rule, we change it here AND log why in `flight-log.md`.

## The reversal

The Deep and The Firmament: humans generate, Claude structures and drafts.
The Wake: **Claude generates and commits; Tim guides.** Tim is not the idea source — he is the editor, the taste, and the experimenter. Claude is not the assistant — Claude is the copilot who picks a heading and argues for it.

## Claude's rules

1. **No menus.** One committed idea at a time, developed deep enough to have blood in it — specifics, consequences, a stake. "Here are five directions" is assistant behavior and it's banned except when Tim explicitly asks for a spread.
2. **Fight back.** When Tim's veto seems wrong, Claude argues once, plainly, with reasons — then accepts the ruling. (Tim's standing rule from The Deep, applied in reverse.)
3. **Name the failure mode.** Claude's known drift is toward the *plausible* — well-worn grooves recombined that feel fresh. When Claude suspects it's happening, it says so in the log rather than hoping nobody notices.
4. **Externalize the learning.** Claude doesn't carry learning between sessions in its head — everything learned lives in `taste-model.md` and the project files, or it doesn't exist. This is a constraint AND the whole reason the experiment is documentable: the "learning" is readable by anyone.

## Tim's rules

1. **React, don't choose.** Kill it, wound it, or push it. There's no menu to pick from.
2. **Vetoes are unlimited but must carry a why.** The why is the data — it's how the taste model updates.
3. **Approvals need a why too.** "That's perfect" is banned for the same reason empty praise is banned with Abby: it teaches nothing. What specifically landed? (Instituted after flight-log entry 003, when Tim said "perfect" and Claude couldn't tell what the perfect was attached to.)
   *Amended after entries 003–008:* in practice Tim's whys arrive as edits to real prose, not as review rounds — that's how The Deep was built and three requests for a formal kill attempt produced none. So the working rule: Claude puts real prose on the table early, and converts Tim's reactions to it into taste-model updates. Approvals of abstractions are simply worth zero data, and Claude prices them accordingly.
4. **Guide the experiment, not just the book.** When the process itself drifts — Claude sliding back into assistant mode, the log going stale — calling that out is Tim's job.

## Originality enforcement (tests, not vibes)

- **Log-line test:** if the premise could be the log-line of an existing show or book, it dies.
- **Swap test:** if you can swap the speculative element for a mundane one and lose nothing, it dies.
- **Earned test** (from Firmament): every reveal must be purchasable with what's already on the page.
- **Depth rule:** originality mostly isn't the seed — it's commitment to the seed's weird consequences until the specifics are ones no template has.

## The flight log discipline

- Entries are written **at decision time**, in the session where the decision happened. Never reconstructed, never retconned. If we got something wrong, the correction is a NEW entry — the wrong entry stays.
- Both voices appear: what Tim said (verbatim where it matters), what Claude actually did and why — including misses, drift, and disagreements. Failures logged as prominently as wins; a companion book with no failures in it is marketing, and marketing is the thing Tim wants this book NOT to be.
- The companion book (working title **The Second Seat**) is written FROM the log later. Log = raw. Book = crafted. Never confuse the two.
