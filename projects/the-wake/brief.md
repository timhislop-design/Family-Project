# The Wake — Premise (Committed)

**Status:** Committed by Claude, July 24, 2026. Greenlit by Tim same day ("I love that" / "The wake is a good premise" — reasons still unharvested; per flight-log 008 the taste data now comes from his edits to real prose). First clay: `drafts/2026-07-24_v1/opening.md`.

## Theme (committed Jul 24 — Tim's directive: "stories that have meaning for people")

**Witness.** Against a universe too old and too big to have ever noticed us, the unglamorous discipline of paying attention — keeping the light, writing the log no one reads — is how small lives end up mattering. Significance isn't scale; it's faithfulness. The rule: meaning must be load-bearing, never installed. This book's plot literally enacts its theme — the only instrument that ever measured the truth is a century of unread, faithfully kept logs. And it rhymes on purpose with the meta-project: the flight log IS a keeper's log; The Second Seat is Tim doing what the Hallorans do. One theme, both books.

## Protagonist (committed)

**Eli Halloran, 16.** Last generation of the family that keeps Halloran Station — lighthouse tradition: the light carries the keeper's name, "the Halloran Light." Assigned the handover grunt-work: digitizing the family archive under a DISPOSAL — CLASS C sticker. Knows the folklore cold and is embarrassed by it. Family: Dad (stiff pragmatist who half-mocks Gran's "superstition" — and has been secretly continuing her margin-marks for years), a sister, Gran dead but everywhere in the logs. House pattern holds: anxious first, then rises.

## Audience (committed Jul 24, on Tim's ask)

Grown-up science fiction written so a sharp thirteen-year-old can read it straight through — the band where a kid and a grandparent read the same pages and neither is condescended to. Clean by house rules, zero dumbing down. Shelf-neighbors: The Expanse, The Martian, Becky Chambers. Reason: the buyers largely arrive as adults through the Second Seat story, but the house voice, Eli at 16, Abby's open door, and the theme of witness all argue for the all-ages register.

## Chapter One shape (committed)

- **Open:** the scanning scene (v1 opening) — the sticker, the logs, Gran's fish-hook margin marks, "answered before sent."
- **Middle:** station life warm and specific; the handover notice; the replacement unit due on the next supply run; family friction over what the archive is worth.
- **Turn:** the joke chart — sixty years of fish-hooks meant to prove Gran was losing it. The line comes out straight. The last point is three weeks old, in Dad's handwriting.
- **Close:** Eli tells no one — and steals one logbook back off the disposal crate. He becomes a keeper the moment keeping officially ends.
- Discipline: the wake is never named in chapter one. Nothing cosmic is explained. The book earns it slow.

## The premise

The solar system is a healed wound. The asteroid belt isn't a failed planet, Saturn's rings aren't captured ice, our absurdly oversized Moon isn't luck — something passed through here once, at a scale where planets are spray, so long ago the scar looks like nature. Humanity doesn't know. We evolved in the splash.

Strung across the outer system is a chain of crewed relay beacons, kept by families for generations — a law written in blood, after an automated relay once let a distress call rot in its queue for nine hours. Every keeper family has its folklore: grandmother's drift measurements that never resolved, the light that answers a half-second early once a decade. A century of it, dismissed as lighthouse superstition.

**The weld:** the keepers' logs are the only continuous record long enough to see the scar still moving. Now automation is finally replacing the keepers, the logs are about to become landfill, and one keeper's kid — doing the humiliating work of digitizing the family's "junk" for the handover — assembles a hundred and forty years of superstition into one line on one chart. And the line points somewhere.

The obsolescence story and the cosmic story are the same story: the thing being thrown away as sentimental is the only instrument that ever measured the truth. Kitchen-table scale on a deep-time canvas. The disaster isn't coming — it already happened, before anything could remember it. The dread is that wakes have a direction.

## The three attack vectors (Tim: aim here)

1. **Does the weld hold?** Or is this a cozy book stapled to an awe book and called structure?
2. **Deep time as antagonist means nothing chases anyone.** No Phorcys, no high priest, no countdown. Fatal engine problem, or the point?
3. **One close POV in a kitchen is a lot of restraint for a premise this big.** Is the small aperture discipline, or a waste of the Expanse-scale canvas?

## Differentiation

- Not The Expanse: no protomolecule spectacle, no system-wide war, no multi-POV. The alien element is an *absence* — evidence of a passage, not a presence.
- Not The Deep: no hidden people, no factions-over-secrecy. The secret here isn't kept by anyone; it's just too slow for anyone to see.
- Not The Firmament: nobody built this box. There's no warden, no schedule, no liturgy. The truth isn't sealed — it's scattered in plain sight across a century of margins.
- Standing tests (inherited from Firmament's differentiation.md): log-line test (could this be an existing show? then it dies), swap test (replace the sci-fi element with a mundane one and lose nothing? then it dies). The swap-test answer here: swap the wake for anything mundane and the logs become mere nostalgia — the premise dies. It passes.
