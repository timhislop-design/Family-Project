# Edda Halloran — Gran. Died before page one; on every page anyway.

**The fifth keeper, the first witness.** Never appears in scene — no flashbacks (house discipline: drip, don't dump; she exists only through the archive, other people's habits, and the margin). The reader assembles her the way Eli does: handwriting that goes uphill when she's excited, weather logged in a vacuum, the fish-hook notation she invented, a first entry at age eleven that is terrible and never corrected.

**What she actually did:** noticed the early answers, took them seriously, measured them for sixty years, told almost no one after the once she tried — the family memory of *that* (what happened when Edda spoke up, and to whom) is a hole in the record the book walks past twice before falling into it in Act II.

**What she never did:** explain herself. The fish-hooks come with no key. She built the dataset and left the meaning for whoever loved her enough to read all of it. Which is either faith in the family, or the loneliness of being the only believer — the book lets both be true.

**Legacy mechanics:** her pen (refilled, in Rhys's coat), her first entry (quoted Act II), her last volume (the one death interrupted mid-page — the page Rhys finished). Three objects, no ghosts.

**Stake in the ground:** Edda is never vindicated *to* the world in Book 1 — only to her family. The big vindication is series-scale, and she won't be alive for it, which is the theme stated as a life: the witness doesn't get to see it matter. She wrote it down anyway.
