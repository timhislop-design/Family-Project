# THE DEEP — World-Frame (opening pages)
*(Draft 6 — THE NARRATOR IS IGGY, grown, looking back after the whole story (three books, give or take). Lighthearted but hardened; fond, wry, carrying scars he doesn't explain. He never names himself — that's a series-level secret. He was THERE, and it leaks through in small ways a rereader will catch. After the handoff, everything is through Nerai's fifteen-year-old eyes, and the narrator does not return. ~3 pages.)*

---

I've had a long time to decide where this story starts, and I keep landing in the same place: with the lie. Every age has its official version of the truth. Ours went like this.

Once, the story says, the world was full of magic, and then it wasn't. The dragons went wherever dragons go. The old gods got quiet. And the strange, gifted few — and they were never a kind, never a category, just one impossible thing per person: a boy who could coax fire like a stray cat (I knew that one personally; he was an idiot, but his heart was in the right place), a girl the water followed, a child who stepped into one shadow and out of another a mile away — they learned to hide. They built their schools behind ordinary walls. They taught their children to pass for human. And the rest of the world was allowed to go on believing it was exactly as small as it looked.

That's the version they teach in the quiet schools, to the quiet children — each one carrying their single strange gift, no two gifts alike, no names for any of it that ever stuck. *This is what you are,* the teachers say, *and this is all there is.* They say it kindly. They say it the way you tell a child there's nothing under the bed — not because it's true, but because it lets everyone sleep. I've said it that way myself, once or twice, back when I still thought sleeping was the point.

I want to be careful here, because I am about to call a great many wise and frightened people liars.

It isn't that their story is wrong. The fire was real. The shadows were real. The schools were real, and the children in them were every bit as remarkable as they'd been told. The story isn't a lie. It's just *small.* It's the truth the way a tide pool is the ocean — accurate, and complete, and a thousand miles short of the deep.

Because here's the part they left out. The part that fit on no school wall, that no kind teacher would say out loud:

We were never the first.

Long before the first human stood up and decided the world was its to name, the first people were already old. They never crawled out of the sea to build their cities in the sun. They stayed. They have always stayed — down in the cold and the dark and the enormous quiet, in the deep places no light has reached and no school has ever mapped — watching us learn to walk, and learn to lie, and build our small true stories about exactly how strange the world is allowed to be.

You should understand what they are, before you decide how to feel about them. They are not monsters, whatever the old sailors carved into their maps. They are not gods, whatever the drowned cults sang. I've met them. They're people — older people, patient people, who grew their cities instead of building them, coaxing light out of living things until the deep glowed like a sky turned upside down. They speak in a language that travels through water the way nothing travels through air, more song than speech, and they remember. That is the thing to know about them above everything else: they *remember.* They were there for the first sunrise anyone bothered to notice. They watched the ice come and go. They had been listening to us, patiently, for longer than we'd had a word for *patience.*

And in all that time, through all that watching, they kept one law above every other. The oldest rule, the one even their children knew before they could swim straight: *do not interrupt.* Let the surface become whatever the surface is going to become. Do not rise. Do not reveal. Do not reach up out of the dark to steady a world that did not ask to be steadied. To them this restraint was the highest thing they had — proof that they were wiser than the noise above them.

But here's what nobody tells you about a rule that old. The longer you keep it, the more it starts to look like a question. And somewhere in the last hundred years — as our cities lit up the night, as our voices crossed their oceans on cables thin as eels, as we sent our own small lights out past the moon — the question got loud enough to split them in two.

One side said: *never.* The surface would never be ready; to rise was to be feared, and hunted, and ended, and they would not trade the safety of the deep for the gratitude of animals that had learned to talk. They were called, by those who didn't love them, the cold ones. They called themselves careful. Both things were true. Most true things come in pairs like that.

The other side said: *now.* That the waiting had curdled into its own kind of cowardice. That we were closer to understanding than we had ever been. That a secret kept too long stops being wisdom and starts being a wound. That it was, at long last, about time.

And lately — by their counting, a blink — the argument had begun to spill. Lights where no lights should be. Shapes on sonar that got laughed off the news. A fisherman who told the truth to a camera once and was never taken seriously again; I've thought about that man a lot over the years. The careful ones called it treason. The impatient ones called it practice. Either way, the world above had started dreaming about the deep again, and it did not know why.

There was a third thing, too, which neither side would say in the open, so I'll say it here. Something in the deep had begun to let go. The light that grew for ten thousand years was growing thinner. The song came back wrong in places, like an echo off a wall that had moved. Half of them said it was nothing. The other half had started looking up. I won't tell you what it was. I'm not sure, even now, that anyone fully knows. But keep it in your pocket while you read, the way they kept it in theirs: quietly, and all the time.

I'll let you decide which side was right. You'll have help — because someone is about to walk you down to where that argument lives. Not an elder. Not a general. A girl. One who was born in the dark and sent up into the light by her own people — for her safety, they said, though I'm not certain they believed it themselves. She was born, as it happens, in the first year the deep shivered, and old people do not forgive a coincidence like that. She had spent three hundred and four days pretending to be an ordinary strange child in an ordinary strange school, counting the mornings, never once told whether she'd been sent up to come home again or simply sent away. She belonged, fully and painfully, to neither side of the water.

Her name is Nerai. She was fifteen years old that night. She did not yet know what she was — they made sure of that — and she was about to find out the way the truest things are always found out: too fast, and too late, and all at once.

She was the first crack in a secret older than your whole species. She'd hate that sentence, by the way. She'd say nobody is a crack in anything. She'd be wrong, and also right. She usually was.

One more thing, and then I'll get out of the way, because this was never my story to stand in front of. Not everyone in this story gets to stay in it. Get attached anyway. I did.

Let me tell you about the night the sea stepped back.

I remember it better than any night of my life. It started, the way the best and worst things always do, with somebody who couldn't sleep.
