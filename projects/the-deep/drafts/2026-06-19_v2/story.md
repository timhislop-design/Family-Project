# THE DEEP
*(working title)*

---

## The Premise

Every story humans whisper about the strange and gifted is true — the powered kids, the vampires, the shifters, the schools that quietly take them in. But all of it is small compared to the truth held under the sea. The first people never left the water. They've lived in the deep oceans since before there was anything to come back to, watching, waiting, divided.

One faction — the **Abyssals** — says humanity will never be ready, that to surface is to be feared, hunted, ended. The other — the **Pelagics** — says the waiting has gone on too long, that the world is finally close enough to understanding to meet them. And a handful of their children have already slipped ashore, enrolled quietly in the schools meant for the *other* exceptional kids, hiding in plain sight among classmates who think they've seen everything strange the world has to offer.

They haven't.

---

## Chapter One — Dry Land

Three hundred and four days on land, and Nerai still woke with her hands open, reaching for water that wasn't there.

She lay on top of the blankets. She couldn't sleep under them — the weight was wrong, too light and too close at the same time, nothing like the deep, where the whole ocean held you and you never once had to ask. Through the window she could see the seam where the school grounds ended and the sea began, a flat gray line in the dark. Close enough to smell on the wind. Too far to reach without being seen.

A brass key sat on the windowsill. Small, furred green with old salt. She'd worked it out of a wreck when she was little, and kept it, though she had no door and never would. Some nights she held it just to hold something a human hand had once turned without thinking — something from the world she wasn't allowed to want. Tonight she only looked at it.

*Stay small,* her mother had told her, the last time. *Learn them. Never let them learn there is one more.* That had been the whole goodbye. No arms. No hand against her cheek. Her people didn't spend warmth on their children; mostly they didn't spend it at all. Nerai had told herself for three hundred and four days that she didn't need it. The key on the sill said otherwise, and she was tired of arguing with it.

Down the hall, someone laughed — one of the fire kids, the ones who could hold a flame in a cupped palm like it was nothing. The school was full of remarkable children, every one of them sure they'd already met everything strange the world had to offer.

None of them had ever wondered if there was something stranger than themselves. Nerai wondered all the time. It was the thing that kept getting her in trouble.

A knock — two soft beats — and the door opened before she answered. That was Iggy. He never quite managed to wait.

"You're up." A small flame drifted above his open palm, lazy, no bigger than a marble; he carried it around at night the way other kids carried a phone, just for the light. He looked at her a beat too long, the way he did, and then pretended he hadn't. "You're always up."

"I don't sleep much."

"Yeah." He didn't say the rest — that he'd noticed, that he'd started leaving his own door open so he'd hear if she went wandering. He just came in, and the little flame threw soft, moving shadows up the wall. "Something's wrong down at the shore. Reyes is losing it. The tide's going out and not coming back — way out, like the ocean took a step back to look at something." He grinned, because to him it was an adventure. "There's fish on dry sand a half mile out. You have to come see."

The flame above his hand guttered.

Nerai had gone to the window without deciding to. Out past the rooftops, the gray line had pulled back from the land like a held breath, baring the dark floor of a place that was never meant to be looked at in open air.

She knew what an outgoing tide was. This was not that.

"Nerai?" The grin was gone from Iggy's voice. "You okay? You look like you've seen a—"

"Don't," she said softly, "say ghost."

A ghost would have been easy. The whole school knew what to do about ghosts.

She was moving before she'd thought about it — bare feet on cold tile, past Iggy, out the door. He said her name twice, and then, because he was the kind of boy who did the loyal and foolish thing, he followed. The corridor lights stuttered as she passed beneath them. That happened near her sometimes, when she forgot to be careful. She'd stopped being careful about four seconds ago.

Back home, children were taught to wait — to hold still until someone older told them what a thing meant and how they were allowed to feel about it. Her friends were good at waiting. Nerai never had been. The girl who asked. The girl who said it out loud. It had always felt like something wrong with her. Tonight, with every careful person in the building about to freeze, it was the only useful thing she had.

"Where are you going?" Iggy caught her at the stairs, the flame gone now, just a boy in pajama pants chasing a girl toward something neither of them could name. "Reyes said stay inside. There's a *protocol* —"

"Not for this." She pushed through the door to the yard, and the night came in wet and huge, smelling of salt and silt and cold, far-down places. And underneath it — coming up faint through the soles of her bare feet, out of the ground itself — a sound. Low. Patient. A single note held so long it had become the shape of the dark.

She knew that sound. She had been sung to sleep by it.

It was a horn. And far out across the water, something was answering it.

Where the seabed lay bare, where there should have been only wet sand and dying fish, the dark was standing up. Not waves. *Shapes* — tall, unhurried, rising out of the trench in a long slow line, more of them than she had ever been allowed to see in one place in her life. Their lights came on one by one, the way a city wakes, far under a sea that wasn't there to cover them anymore.

Her people. Rising. And somewhere in that line — she knew it the way you know a voice through a wall, without being told — her father, coming ashore at last.

Her eyes stung. She let them.

"Nerai." Iggy had gone very still beside her. "What are they?"

She didn't look away from the water. For three hundred and four days she had kept herself small, and quiet, and careful, and not once had she stopped missing them.

"They're mine," she said.

And then, because Iggy was the only person on dry land who had ever left his door open for her, she reached over without looking and held on to his sleeve — so that whatever was coming, she wouldn't be standing on the line alone.
