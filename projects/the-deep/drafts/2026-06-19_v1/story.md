# THE DEEP
*(working title)*

---

## The Premise

Every story humans whisper about the strange and gifted is true — the powered kids, the vampires, the shifters, the schools that quietly take them in. But all of it is small compared to the truth held under the sea. The first people never left the water. They've lived in the deep oceans since before there was anything to come back to, watching, waiting, divided.

One faction — the **Abyssals** — says humanity will never be ready, that to surface is to be feared, hunted, ended. The other — the **Pelagics** — says the waiting has gone on too long, that the world is finally close enough to understanding to meet them. And a handful of their children have already slipped ashore, enrolled quietly in the schools meant for the *other* exceptional kids, hiding in plain sight among classmates who think they've seen everything strange the world has to offer.

They haven't.

## Chapter One — Dry Land

Nerai had been dry for three hundred and four days, and she still hated it.

Dry was the wrong word, the human word, and it didn't reach far enough. Dry was a towel, a desert, a throat after running. What Nerai felt was something the surface had no name for — the slow, constant ache of a body learning to want air it had never needed, of skin that remembered pressure and got, instead, the thin nothing of a dormitory at two in the morning.

She lay on top of the covers because blankets felt like being caught in a net. Through the window, past the school's black rooftops, she could just see it: a flat gray line where the campus ended and the ocean began. Close enough to smell on the wind. Far enough to be a punishment.

*Stay small,* her mother had told her, the last time, in the language that moved through water better than it ever would through air. *Learn them. Let them love their monsters and their wizards. Never let them learn there is one more.*

That had been the whole goodbye. No hand against her face, no warmth pressed into her the way the surface mothers did it in the films the school showed — her people didn't spend those things on the young. An instruction, the cold, and the long swim up alone. Nerai had told herself for three hundred and four days that she didn't mind. On the windowsill in front of her sat the thing that proved she was lying: a small brass key, green with the sea, pulled from a wreck when she was little and kept ever since, for a door she had never seen, in a house she was not allowed to want.

Down the hall, someone laughed — one of the firelighters, the kids who could hold a flame in a cupped palm and not blink. The school was full of them. Fire and shadow and the ones who could move things with a look. Brilliant, all of them, and so proud of being secret that they'd never once wondered if there were secrets bigger than their own.

Nerai had wondered. That was the trouble with her.

She slid off the bed, crossed to the window, and pressed two fingers against the cold glass. Where she touched it, frost bloomed and then beaded and ran, the water answering her the way it always did, eager, like a dog that had missed her. She watched a single drop trace down the pane and thought about how easy it would be. The window. The dark. The line of gray. Twenty minutes of running and she could be home, and the Abyssals would fold her back in and say *we told you, we told you the surface would break her heart.*

And the Pelagics — her father's faction — would say nothing at all. Which was worse.

A knock. Soft, two beats, then the door opening before she'd answered, because that was the kind of school it was.

"You're awake." It was Iggy, the boy from the room across the hall, backlit by the corridor's amber light, a flame the size of a marble drifting lazily above his open hand. He used it like a nightlight. He thought it was a small thing. "You're always awake."

"I don't sleep much," Nerai said.

"Yeah, I noticed." He stepped in, uninvited, and the little flame threw moving shadows up the walls. "There's something happening at the shore. Coach Reyes is freaking out. They're saying the water's doing something weird — pulling back, way out, like a tide that forgot how to come in." He grinned, because to him this was a thrill, a mystery, a Tuesday. "Whole beach is just sand and fish flopping around. You should see it."

The flame guttered. The air in the room had gone cold and still.

Nerai didn't turn from the window. Out past the rooftops, the gray line had moved — drawn back from the land like a held breath, baring the dark floor of a world that was never meant to be seen in air.

They weren't waiting anymore.

"Nerai?" Iggy's voice had lost its grin. "Hey. You okay? You look like you've seen a—"

"Don't," she said quietly, "say ghost."

Because ghosts would have been easy. Ghosts, this school could handle.

She was already moving — bare feet on cold tile, out the door, past Iggy, who said her name twice and then, when she didn't stop, did the loyal and stupid thing and followed. The corridor lights stuttered as she passed under them. That happened, near her, when she forgot to be careful. She'd stopped being careful about four seconds ago.

Down in the deep, the other children her age were taught to wait — to hold still until an elder told them what a thing meant and how they were allowed to feel about it. Her friends were good at it. They took their parents' side of every argument the way you'd take a coat that was handed to you, and when the water got loud they went quiet and let the older voices fill it. Nerai had never managed the trick. She'd been in trouble for it her whole life: the girl who asked, who said the thing out loud, who couldn't keep still inside her own head. It had always felt like a flaw. Tonight, with the sea standing up out past the rooftops and every careful person in the building about to freeze, it was the only useful thing about her.

"Where are you going?" Iggy caught up at the stairwell, his flame snuffed now, just a kid in pajama pants chasing a girl toward something he couldn't name. "Reyes said stay in the dorms. There's, like, a *protocol* —"

"There's no protocol for this." She hit the door to the yard and the night came in wet and enormous, smelling of salt and silt and the cold deep places, and underneath it — faint, threading up through the soles of her feet from the ground itself — a sound. Low. Patient. A note held so long it had become the shape of the dark.

She knew that sound. She had been sung to sleep by that sound.

It was a horn, and it was being answered.

Across the bared seabed, where there should have been only wet sand and dying fish, the dark was standing up. Not waves — *shapes*, tall and unhurried, rising out of the trench in a long slow line, more of them than she had ever been allowed to see together in her life, their lights coming on one by one like a city waking under the water that wasn't there anymore.

The Pelagics had stopped waiting. And somewhere in that rising line, she knew, without being able to say how, the way you know a voice in the next room — her father was coming ashore.

Iggy made a small sound beside her. "Nerai," he whispered. "What *are* they?"

She found she was crying, and smiling, and terrified, all at the exact same time, which she would later learn was simply what it felt like when the thing you had been told to fear your whole life turned out to be the thing you had been homesick for.

"They're mine," she said.
