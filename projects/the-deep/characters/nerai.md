# Nerai — Backstory (draft to build on)

## Temperament (Tim, Jul 10, 2026 — the pattern that defines her)
**Strong but anxious.** Self-doubt runs constant commentary in her head — she second-guesses, spirals, is sure she's about to be found out. But when the moment actually comes, she **rises, with confidence** — and it surprises her every time. The order matters on the page: dread first, doubt first, then the step forward anyway. She's not fearless; she's scared and moves anyway, and only afterward realizes she sounded sure. Never write her as cool under pressure — write her as certain only in the exact seconds that count.

*Our protagonist. The small one against the big thing — like David: not the strongest, not the chosen, just the one who can't look away from the giant everyone else is afraid of. Here the giant isn't a person. It's the gap between two worlds, and Nerai is the only one homesick for both sides of it.*

## Who she is
Nerai was born in the deep, in the dark cities her people have kept since before humans had a word for the sea. She is young by their counting — they live long, slow lives — which makes her, to the elders, barely more than a child with too many questions. She has never needed air. She has never been warm in the way a surface person means it. And for as long as she can remember, she has pressed herself against the boundary of her own world and *longed for the other one* — the one she was told she could never live in.

She is also a student — sent ashore to the school, but a student her whole life before that too, raised in the deep's long apprenticeship of watching and listening. School is the one place the surface and her own world overlap: a room full of exceptional kids, all of them shaped by parents they didn't choose, all of them trying to figure out which of their parents' beliefs are actually their own.

## What she looks like *(Abby's + Tim's, adopted Jul 10, 2026)*
**Dark hair** — near-black, long, with something not-quite-brown in it underwater light would explain. **Petite but strong** — small-framed, compact, and stronger than she has any right to look; a body built by pressure and current, not by gym class. People underestimate her carry-weight constantly. It's useful.
Ashore, Nerai passes — but only just. There's something in her face that reads as *almost*: eyes set a little wide, features a little sharp, the general look old mythologies gave their mermaids without knowing why. Nobody at school would say "fish." They'd say *striking*, or *where is she from?*, and look a half-second too long. It's a body-level secret to go with the power that leaks — one more thing she has to hold in, one more reason mirrors are complicated. (Rule: never obvious, never explained; it shows up in how other people look at her, not in description dumps.)

## The rumor she was born under *(Abby's, adopted Jul 10, 2026)*
The deep's troubles (see the failing deep, `brief.md`) began — or were first noticed — around the time Nerai was born. Old cultures don't let a coincidence like that lie. Rumor and superstition attached to her early: whispers of a *prophesied end*, a child tangled up in it somehow. **Important: it's not a real prophecy — it's a misreading.** Whether she has anything to do with the failing at all is a series-length question. But the whispers are one more true reason her parents feared what she was and sent her up — and one more inherited story for Nerai to refuse.

## Her parents — important, and far away
Nerai's parents are *important.* In the deep, that doesn't mean warm. Her people are an old, cold-watered culture that does not coddle its young — children are not embraced and reassured; they are *expected.* Affection is not how love is shown down there, if it's shown at all. Her mother and father both hold weight on their factions, and that standing has always mattered more than the small girl trying to get their attention. Nerai grew up admired-of, spoken-about, and quietly unheld. The longing she feels for the surface is, in part, a longing for a kind of closeness her own world doesn't offer.

## Her longing (the engine)
The surface is forbidden, and Nerai is in love with it. Not the way you love something you have; the way you love something you can only watch. As a small girl she followed the hulls of ships the way other children chased lights. She collected sunk things — a shoe, a coin, a page of a book she couldn't read but kept anyway, the writing like a language of bones. She wanted to *understand* humans, not rule them, not fear them. Just know what it was to live up there in the dry and the noise and the light.

That curiosity is her gift and her trouble. Her people survive by being unseen. Nerai survives by needing, badly, to see.

## How her people live
The deep ones don't build like humans; they grow their cities — coral and bioluminescence, structures that glow with living light and answer to song. They speak in a language that moves through water better than air ever could, more music than words. They have long memory; they remember the surface from before there were people on it. They are not monsters and not gods. They are simply older, and they have watched humankind the whole way up from the mud.

## Their oldest law: do not interrupt
For all that watching, they have one sacred rule — **do not interrupt mankind.** Humanity must become whatever it's going to become on its own. To surface, to reveal themselves, to *help* even, is to bend a story that isn't theirs to bend. This restraint is their pride. It is also, some of the young ones think, their cowardice.

## The factions (her family is the fault line)
- **The Abyssals** keep the old law. *We wait. We are not their saviors and not their doom. The world will never truly be ready.* Nerai's **mother** is one of them — and her mother gave Nerai the *official* reason for going ashore: *learn humans quietly, so we never have to meet them at all.* (The truth both parents buried is darker and sadder — see "Why she's ashore" below.)
- **The Pelagics** have lost their patience. *The waiting has become its own kind of harm. Mankind is closer to understanding than it has ever been. It's time.* Nerai's **father** is one of them — and that disagreement split her family long before it split the sea.

So Nerai carries the argument inside her own blood. The mother who says *stay hidden.* The father who says *it's time.* And a girl who, secretly, has always agreed with the side that frightens her most.

## Her friends — and the thing that sets her apart
Without much warmth at home, Nerai leans hard on her friends. They're the family she chose: a small circle of deep-born kids and a few of the school's exceptional ones, the people she actually tells things to. But there's a difference between her and them, and it's the heart of who she is.

Her friends *follow.* They believe what their parents believe, take the faction they were born into, repeat the lines they were raised on — not because they're weak, but because that's what their culture asks of the young: defer, wait, fall in line. When the pressure comes, they go quiet. They don't have a voice yet. Maybe they never will.

**Nerai has one.** She questions. She says the thing out loud that everyone else only thinks. She'll tell her own friends, gently, that they're parroting their parents — and tell the elders the same, less gently. It's gotten her in trouble her whole life. It's also the reason that when her people finally rise and everyone else freezes or obeys, *she's* the one who can speak — for the friends who can't, for the two worlds that won't talk to each other. Her voice is her power, long before anyone hands her another one.

## Her ability — the secret they keep "for her"
Nerai has something no other deep-one has. (Working idea, adjustable: where everyone else's song only shapes water, *hers shapes the line between water and air itself* — she can make the two worlds touch. The frost blooming under her fingers, the corridor lights stuttering when she's stirred up, the sea answering her like a dog that missed her — those aren't her being talented. They're leaks. Her power is the very thing her people's oldest law forbids: she *interrupts.*)

Whatever its exact shape, two things are fixed:
- It starts **out of balance.** It surges and slips when she's torn between the two worlds — which is always. Learning to *hold it* is the work of the series.
- **Her parents know.** And here's the thing that breaks the faction lines: her Abyssal mother and her Pelagic father, who agree on nothing, agree on *this* — to **hide what she is.** Each faction would use her (the Pelagics as living proof the worlds can join; the Abyssals as a danger to be buried). So the people who never held her close suddenly close ranks to keep her secret — and call it love. "We're hiding it to help you."

## The cruel irony (the engine of everything)
The hiding doesn't keep her safe. It teaches her that the people she trusts most will look her in the eye and lie. That's what makes Nerai a **seeker of truth** — not curiosity anymore, but a wound. She will spend the whole series pulling threads she was never meant to touch, precisely *because* they were hidden from her. They thought concealment would protect her. It made her unstoppable, and a little dangerous, and impossible to lie to twice.

## Why she's ashore — the 304 days
The story she was given is the one the deep tells about any child sent up: *you're going to learn the humans, quietly, so we can understand them without ever having to meet them.* A mission. An honor, almost. That's what her mother's *stay small, learn them* was dressed up as.

It isn't the real reason. The real reason is older and harder, and nobody said it out loud.

Nerai was different, and her difference was starting to show. The water answered her when it shouldn't. Cold and stutters and small impossible things gathered around her when she felt too much. And she *questioned* — out loud, in a culture where children are supposed to wait to be told what to think. To the elders she was a leak waiting to happen, a secret that wouldn't stay a secret. To the factions she was a prize or a danger, depending on who reached her first.

And her parents — this is the part that complicates everything — were not simply cruel. They were, in their quiet way, among the ones who had begun to *doubt.* They had looked at their people's whole careful, frightened, hidden way of living and felt, privately, that it could not hold forever — that a people cannot stay scared in the dark for all of time. In another life they might have been the ones to lead their world up into the light.

But it is one thing to doubt the old ways in the abstract, and another to hold the proof of their ending in your arms and not be afraid of it. Nerai *was* that proof — the future they suspected was coming, breathing and stubborn and impossible to keep small. And she scared them. So they did the thing frightened people do even when they love you: they sent the frightening thing away. Up. Ashore. Out of the dark where someone might notice — and they called it protection, and almost meant it.

That's the wound under the wound: not that her parents didn't love her, but that they loved her *and* feared her, and the fear is the part she felt. She counts the days because she still doesn't know which it was — exile or errand, sent up to come back or sent up to be gone. Three hundred and four mornings, and no one has told her which.

## The wound
She belongs fully to neither world. Too curious for the deep, too strange for the surface. Homesick for the ocean *and* for a land she's never been allowed to call home. Admired by parents who never held her; loved by friends who can't speak up for themselves. That's the ache that makes her move when everyone else stays still — and it's why, when her people finally rise, she's the one standing right on the line between them, with the only voice that carries.
