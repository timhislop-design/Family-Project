# THE DEEP — The True History
*(The mythology bible. The idea: every flood myth, fish-god, and drowned city humanity remembers is a garbled memory of the first people. We're not inventing a mythology — we're claiming the real ones. This document is for US; almost none of it ever appears on the page directly. See "How this reaches the page" at the bottom.)*

---

## The spine, in one paragraph
The first people did not always live in the abyss. Through the last ice age they held the coasts and warm shallow seas — and for a while, at the water's edge, they *taught* the young species watching them from the beaches. Then the world broke: ice melted too fast, the seas rose and swallowed every coast on Earth, and their shallow civilization drowned. Humanity remembers this as the Flood. The first people remember it as the Shattering — and what came after: worship, then fear, then hunting. So they went all the way down, grew the true deep cities, and made the law — *do not interrupt* — sealing themselves away with the one thing that made the abyss livable. Everything humans call myth is the residue of the ten thousand years before the door closed. And now, the thing that has kept the abyss livable ever since... is failing.

## The timeline

### 1. Deep time — the first people
Older than us by an unfathomable margin. They watched the ice come and go (canon, prologue). Their civilization was always *grown*, not built — living light, song-shaped water — but before the Shattering it reached up into the shallows and the warm coastal terraces, close enough to the surface to be *seen*.

### 2. The Age of Teaching (before ~12,900 years ago)
The one great interruption. At the edges of the drowned-to-be coasts, emissaries came ashore and taught the clever new species: writing's ancestors, seed-keeping, law, the stars.

**What humanity kept of it:**
- **Oannes** — the Babylonian fish-man who rose from the sea each morning to teach civilization and returned to the water each night. Recorded by Berossus. This is a deep-one emissary, remembered almost *accurately*.
- **The Apkallu** — Sumer's seven sages, sent by **Enki**, god of the deep waters, who lived in the **Abzu** — the freshwater abyss. (Some scholars trace the very word *abyss* toward Abzu. Either way, look at our faction name: **Abyssals**. The deep ones named themselves after their own address; humans turned the address into a god's.)
- **Dagon**, fish-god of the Philistines. **The "water tribes" memory** carried by sea peoples — the Sama-Bajau (Abby's pick) as descendants of coastal humans who lived *closest* to the teaching shore, and never fully forgot.

### 3. The Shattering (the Younger Dryas, ~12,900–11,700 years ago)
The real-world event: the abrupt climate catastrophe at the end of the ice age — temperatures whiplashing, meltwater pulses raising the seas, whole inhabited coastlines drowned (Sundaland in the Indonesian shelf, Doggerland under the North Sea). Cause still argued over — comet debris, collapsing ice dams. **In our world: the cause is a mystery to the deep ones too. This matters later.**

For humans it was the end of the world. For the first people it was worse — the shallow terraces, the teaching shores, the reachable cities: all destroyed or drowned beyond use, and the sea itself turned violent and strange.

**What humanity kept of it:**
- **The Flood** — Noah, Utnapishtim and Atrahasis, Deucalion, Manu. Every culture keeps a flood myth because it happened on every coast. And in the Mesopotamian versions, one god breaks ranks to *warn* a chosen family — Enki, whispering through a reed wall. That's the deep ones' last great act on the surface: they saw the water coming (they feel the ocean like we feel weather) and some of them warned the neighbors. The arks are remembered because they were real, and they were *advised*.
- **Atlantis** — Plato dates its sinking to ~9,600 BC: almost exactly the end of the Younger Dryas. Humans remember a drowned advanced city and assume it was human. It wasn't. Atlantis is the garbled memory of *their* greatest shallow capital.
- **Genesis 1:2** — "darkness was over the face of *the deep*." The Hebrew word is **tehom**, cognate with **Tiamat**, Babylon's primordial sea. The oldest human scriptures open by pointing at our setting. The book's title has been hiding in plain sight for three thousand years.

### 4. The Withdrawal and the Law
Here's the tragedy that makes the law *earned* instead of arbitrary: the warning was repaid with worship. The drowned cults (canon, prologue). Then worship curdled — gods get blamed for floods, and survivors' children hunted the shore-walkers their grandparents had knelt to. The deep ones drew the obvious, wounded conclusion: *every time we touch them, it ends in ruin — theirs or ours.*

So they went down. All the way. They grew the true deep cities in the places no light reaches, sustained by [the thing that is now failing — see below], and made the oldest law: **do not interrupt.** The prologue's "restraint as their highest pride" is a scar tissue reading of their own history.

**What humanity kept of it:** diminishing glimpses. **Poseidon** and his court — the memory of their power, relocated to a god. **Leviathan** and the sea serpents on the old maps — their bonded great-beasts, glimpsed at patrol (→ Abby's sea-monster mounts and her left-behind pet now have a mythic pedigree). **Mermaids** — wide-set eyes, sharp features, seen for a second in the water (→ Nerai's face). **Jonah**, **selkies**, **the Nephilim option** (if we take the "hidden few know" path: "sons of God came in to the daughters of men" reads very differently).

### 5. Now — the failing
The thing that made the abyss livable after the Withdrawal — grown, sung, ancient, taken utterly for granted — is letting go (canon, `brief.md`, Abby's idea). And here's how the mythology pays off: **the deep ones don't know what caused the Shattering either.** They never found out. They just survived it and built lower. So when the deep begins failing without warning, their oldest unanswered question comes back up out of the dark: *is it happening again?* The clock that started at the Younger Dryas may be a cycle, not an event. "It's about time" stops being a slogan and becomes literal.

And Nerai was born the year it started shivering (canon, `characters/nerai.md`).

## The Nereus gift (this one's a jackpot)
Nerai's name has been sitting on a Greek myth this whole time. **Nereus**, the Old Man of the Sea — father of the Nereids, the sea-nymphs. Hesiod says of him: *true and never lies*... *unerring and kind, who never forgets what is right.* The gentle, TRUTHFUL old god of the sea, later shoved aside by Poseidon's louder mythology.

Our girl whose only power is her **voice**, whose engine is **refusing lies**, is named for the sea-god of *truth-telling*. We didn't plan that. We're keeping it. Possible canon: "Nerai" is an old deep name, and the Greeks got Nereus *from* it — one more thing they kept. Her name literally means what her arc is.

## The ruins are older than the story (decided Jul 10, 2026)
The drowned terraces in the shallows are *not* just the Shattering's wreckage. Some of them are **far older** — older than the Age of Teaching, older than the deep's official history has room for. The deep ones don't discuss this; most have never looked. Which means the ruins are to the deep what the deep is to humanity: proof that the accepted story is too small. (And if something was drowned *before* the last Shattering... the cycle has happened before, and the failing is the clock coming around again.) Book 1: mentioned only — a whisper, a glimpse on a map. The ruins open up later in the series.

## The naming rule (Tim, Jul 10, 2026)
**Names and character development draw from the real record — Bible, Sumer, Greece, the sea myths — because in this world, the myths drew from US.** A deep-one's name should be the *original* that humans garbled into a god or a monster; a human character's name can carry a biblical or mythic echo of their role. The mine: Nereus and the fifty Nereids (Thetis, Galene, Doris, Psamathe, Ligeia…), Enki/Ea and the Abzu, Tiamat/tehom, Nammu, the Apkallu (Adapa, Uanna/Oannes), Leviathan and tannin, Dagon, Atargatis (the first mermaid), Jonah, Miriam, Proteus, Palaemon, Ceto, Pontus, Glaucus, Sedna, Tangaroa, Sama-Bajau names. Rule of thumb: pick the myth that matches the character's JOB in the story, then wear it lightly — "Tiama," not "Tiamat the Dragon of Chaos."

## Choice points (flagged, not locked)
1. **Did they teach on purpose?** Recommended: yes, once — the Age of Teaching — and the law exists because of how it ended. Gives the law a wound instead of a rule.
2. **What caused the Shattering?** Recommended: unknown to everyone, including the deep — and connected to the failing. This is the series-level mystery under Book 1's mystery. (If the failing began at Nerai's birth, and the Shattering was a cycle... the rumor about her gets teeth.)
3. **How much surfaces on the page?** Recommended: almost none, ever, as exposition. See below.

## How this reaches the page (house rules apply)
**Drip, don't dump — this doc is the iceberg; the page gets the tip.** The prologue already has the slots: "whatever the old sailors carved into their maps," "whatever the drowned cults sang." Those lines now have ten thousand years of specific weight behind them and don't need to change a word.

On the page, the mythology arrives only as:
- **Human guesses.** Iggy, watching the sea stand up: *"...so, Atlantis?"* Wrong, and almost right. Human characters reach for Noah, Poseidon, mermaids — and Nerai gets to feel the vertigo of hearing her own history mangled into bedtime stories.
- **Deep-one memory.** Elders who speak of the Shattering the way we'd speak of something that happened to our grandparents. Never a history lesson — a grudge, a grief, a warning.
- **The academy** (answers one of Abby's questions): the gifted schools teach the *small* version of history (canon, prologue). A classroom scene where the "official" myth unit gets the Flood hilariously, painfully wrong — while the one student who knows sits on her hands — is a free scene. It's the book's whole theme in a classroom.
