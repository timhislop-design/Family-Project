THE DEEP

How an Idea Becomes a Book — the premise, and every turn it took to get here

Written for Abby. This is the part most people never get to see: not the finished story, but how it actually got made — one decision at a time, starting from something we literally called "a dumb premise." Nothing here fell out of the sky. We built all of it.

1. Where it started

The spark was simple, and not even original — on purpose. Lots of stories share it: Harry Potter, Avatar: The Last Airbender, Percy Jackson, Star Wars. A hidden world of special kids. Powers. A secret school. We said: fine, all of that is true. And then we added one new thing on top:

What if the oldest secret isn't in a castle or a forest — it's under the ocean? What if the first people never left the water, and they've been down there the whole time, watching us, arguing about whether to finally come up?

That "what if" was the whole seed. We even said the premise was kind of dumb. Dumb is okay. A seed looks like nothing until it grows.

2. The premise now

Every myth humans tell — the kids who command fire and water and wind and shadow, the secret schools that hide them — is real. But it's small next to the truth under the sea: the first people never left the water. They've lived in the deep oceans since before there was anything to come back to, and they're split over one question — should they finally let the surface know they exist? One side says never. The other says it's time. A few of their children are already hidden ashore, at the schools meant for the other strange kids. One of them is about to get caught in the middle of everything.

3. How it grew — decision by decision

This is the real lesson. Watch how many choices turned a seed into a world:

  1. We made the names mean something. Not random — derivative. Nerai comes from Nereid (sea nymphs). Iggy from ignis (fire). The two factions became the Abyssals (the deep, the cautious) and the Pelagics (the upper water, the ones who want to rise).
  2. We found our hero — and made her small. Nerai isn't the strongest or the chosen one. She's like David against Goliath: little, overlooked, but the only one who won't look away from the giant. Here the giant is the gap between two worlds.
  3. We gave her a hard home. Her people are an old, cold culture that doesn't hug its kids. Her parents are important but distant. So her longing for the warm, noisy surface is also a longing for closeness she never got.
  4. We found what makes her special — and it isn't a superpower. It's her voice. Her friends follow their parents and go quiet. Nerai questions. She says the thing out loud. It gets her in trouble — and it's exactly what makes her the hero.
  5. We gave her a secret even she doesn't know. She has a hidden ability (she can touch the line between water and air — she interrupts, the one thing her people's law forbids). Her mother and father — who agree on nothing — both hid it "to help her." And that lie is what turns her into someone who can't stop hunting for the truth.
  6. We turned a book into a series. Love became a slow arc across books — innocent at first, complicated by Book 3, costly later. Not the point of Book 1; a seed for the long game.
  7. We invented someone to push against. A "silver spoon" deep-one who sneers at "land dwellers" — the prejudice Nerai refuses, walking around in a body. (The scariest version: he wants to surface too, but to rule, not to meet.)
  8. We figured out the opening shape. A few voiced pages that lay out the "official" history of the world — and then refuse it ("I'm about to call a great many wise and frightened people liars"). After that, the whole book is through Nerai's eyes only.
  9. We found the theme hiding under all of it. The whole thing is about not accepting the inherited story. The narrator refuses it; Nerai refuses it. The form and the hero became the same idea: disagreement is the engine.

4. Watch the writing find its voice

This is the part Abby should really see. The very first sentence of Chapter One got rewritten several times — same girl, same moment, totally different feeling — until it sounded true:

Try 1 (too grown-up / romance-novel):

"…She was getting good, she decided, at wanting things she wasn't allowed to have. It was about the only thing she was getting good at."

Pretty, but it sounds like a wistful adult, not a kid.

Try 2 (innocent, wondering):

"…She didn't know yet whether that was lonely, or just new."

Closer — younger, curious. But still a little floaty.

Try 3 (the keeper — a real teenager):

"Three hundred and four days on land, and Nerai still hadn't figured out how to look like she belonged."

There she is. Insecure, self-conscious, sure everyone can tell she doesn't fit. That's a feeling every teenager knows in their bones — so now we're with her, not reading at her.

Same sentence, three tries. That's not failure. That's the work.

5. The rule under everything

Read with the reader, never at them. (Think Harry Potter — you discover Hogwarts exactly as Harry does.) The storyteller never knows more than Nerai or stops to explain. We learn the world when she does, feel it when she feels it. The moment a book starts lecturing you, it's reading at you. We don't do that.

6. We kept every version

Nothing got thrown away. Every draft is frozen and saved, so we can always go back. That's the secret confidence of a writer: you can try anything, be as bad as you want, take any risk — because the version you liked is safe, and you can always return to it.

That's the whole magic, Abby: a "dumb" idea, plus a lot of small brave choices, plus the willingness to write it wrong on the way to writing it right.
