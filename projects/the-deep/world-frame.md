# THE DEEP — World-Frame (opening pages)
*(working draft — the voiced opener that runs before Chapter 1. Lays out the "official" history of the world, then refuses to accept it. The storyteller speaks here and ONLY here; after the handoff, everything is through Nerai's eyes. ~3 pages.)*

---

Every age has its official version of the truth. Ours goes like this.

Once, the story says, the world was full of magic, and then it wasn't. The dragons went wherever dragons go. The old gods got quiet. And the strange, gifted few — and they were never a kind, never a category, just one impossible thing per person: a boy who could coax fire like a stray cat, a girl the water followed, a child who stepped into one shadow and out of another a mile away — they learned to hide. They built their schools behind ordinary walls. They taught their children to pass for human. And the rest of the world was allowed to go on believing it was exactly as small as it looked.

That's the version they teach now, in the quiet schools, to the quiet children — each of them carrying their one strange gift, no two gifts alike, no names for any of it that ever stuck. *This is what you are,* the teachers say, *and this is all there is.* They say it kindly. They say it the way you tell a child there is nothing under the bed — not because it's true, but because it lets everyone sleep.

I want to be careful here, because I am about to call a great many wise and frightened people liars.

It isn't that their story is wrong. The fire is real. The shadows are real. The schools are real, and the children in them are every bit as remarkable as they've been told. The story isn't a lie. It's just *small.* It's the truth the way a tide pool is the ocean — accurate, and complete, and a thousand miles short of the deep.

Because here is the part they leave out. Here is the part that fits on no school wall, that no kind teacher will say out loud:

We were never the first.

Long before the first human stood up and decided the world was its to name, the first people were already old. They never crawled out of the sea to build their cities in the sun. They stayed. They have always stayed — down in the cold and the dark and the enormous quiet, in the deep places no light has reached and no school has ever mapped — watching us learn to walk, and learn to lie, and build our small true stories about exactly how strange the world is allowed to be.

You should understand what they are, before you decide how to feel about them. They are not monsters, whatever the old sailors carved into their maps. They are not gods, whatever the drowned cults sang. They are people — older people, patient people, who grew their cities instead of building them, coaxing light out of living things until the deep glowed like a sky turned upside down. They speak in a language that travels through water the way nothing travels through air, more song than speech, and they remember. That is the thing to know about them above all else: they *remember.* They were here for the first sunrise anyone bothered to notice. They watched the ice come and go. They have been listening to us, patiently, for longer than we have had a word for *patience.*

And in all that time, through all that watching, they have kept one law above every other. The oldest rule, the one even their children know before they can swim straight: *do not interrupt.* Let the surface become whatever the surface is going to become. Do not rise. Do not reveal. Do not reach up out of the dark to steady a world that did not ask to be steadied. To them this restraint is the highest thing they have — proof that they are wiser than the noise above them.

But here is what no one tells you about a rule that old. The longer you keep it, the more it starts to look like a question. And somewhere in the last hundred years — as our cities lit up the night, as our voices crossed their oceans on cables thin as eels, as we sent our own small lights out past the moon — the question got loud enough to split them in two.

One side says: *never.* The surface will never be ready; to rise is to be feared, and hunted, and ended, and they will not trade the safety of the deep for the gratitude of animals who learned to talk. They are called, by those who don't love them, the cold ones. They call themselves careful.

The other side says: *now.* That the waiting has curdled into its own kind of cowardice. That we are closer to understanding than we have ever been. That a secret kept too long stops being wisdom and starts being a wound. That it is, at long last, about time.

And lately, the argument has begun to spill. Lights where no lights should be. Shapes on sonar that get laughed off the news. A fisherman who tells the truth to a camera once, and is never taken seriously again. The careful ones call it treason. The impatient ones call it practice. Either way, the world above has started dreaming about the deep again — and it does not know why.

There is a third thing, too, which neither side will say in the open, so I will say it here. For some years now — a blink, by their counting — something in the deep has been letting go. The light that grew for ten thousand years grows thinner. The song comes back wrong in places, like an echo off a wall that has moved. Half of them say it is nothing. The other half have started looking up. I won't tell you what it is. I am not sure they know. But keep it in your pocket while you read, the way they keep it in theirs: quietly, and all the time.

I'll let you decide which of them is right. You'll have help — because someone is about to walk you down to where that argument lives. Not an elder. Not a general. A girl. One who was born in the dark and sent up into the light by her own people — for her safety, they said, though I am not certain they believed it themselves. She was born, as it happens, in the first year the deep shivered — and old people do not forgive a coincidence like that. She has spent three hundred and four days pretending to be an ordinary strange child in an ordinary strange school, counting the mornings, never once told whether she was sent up to come home again or simply sent away. She belongs, fully and painfully, to neither side of the water.

Her name is Nerai. She is fifteen years old. She does not yet know what she is, not really — they made sure of that — and she is about to find out the way the truest things are always found out: too fast, and too late, and all at once.

She is the first crack in a secret older than your whole species.

Let me tell you about the night the sea stepped back.
