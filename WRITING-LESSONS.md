# Writing Lessons

*Things we're learning as we go. Written for Abby — and for Dad, who's learning too. We might throw away every story we test these on. That's fine. The lessons are the keepers.*

---

## 1. First drafts are clay
The first version is never supposed to be good. It's clay — you make it just so you have something to shape. "Cheesy" is fine on a first pass. You fix it by squishing it again, not by being afraid to start.

## 2. Drip, don't dump
Don't stop the story to explain a character's whole past. Hand the reader one small piece at a time, hidden inside something happening *right now*. A brass key on a windowsill can tell you a girl is lonely without the book ever saying "she was lonely."

## 3. Put the feeling in a *thing*, not a label
Weak: "She was sad and missed her mom."
Strong: a goodbye with "no arms, no hand against her cheek." A held sleeve. A door left open.
Show us an object or a gesture and let *us* feel it. Don't tell us what to feel — trust the reader.

## 4. Let the character *choose* — and let it cost
We learn who someone is by what they do when it's hard. The best moment in our chapter is Nerai reaching for Iggy's sleeve. Nobody made her. It shows she's lonely and brave at the same time, in one small action. Choices reveal character better than any description.

## 5. Let the character be *wrong*
Heroes who are always right are boring. The interesting ones misunderstand things, judge too fast, get it wrong — and grow. Room to be wrong is room for a story.

## 6. Write in *her* voice, even in the narration
This is the secret trick (Jane Austen lived on it). Even the parts that aren't dialogue can sound like how the character thinks. "She was tired of arguing with it" isn't a flat fact — it's Nerai's tired, wry voice leaking into the page. The more the storytelling sounds like *her*, the closer we feel to her.

## 7. Read *with* the reader, never *at* them
The big one. (Think Harry Potter — we discover Hogwarts exactly as Harry does.)
- **Reading AT:** a narrator who knows more than the character and explains things to you from above.
- **Reading WITH:** you're glued to the character's shoulder. You learn things when *she* learns them, feel them when *she* feels them. You figure the world out together.
Whenever the storyteller seems to know more than the hero and pauses to explain, that's reading *at*. Cut it. Get back on her shoulder.

## 8. Find her biggest fear — then make it come true *(Abby's rule)*
Figure out the one thing your character is most afraid of. Not spiders — the *real* fear, the one under everything (for Nerai: that she was sent away, not sent out). Then build the story so it happens. A hero only shows you who she is when the thing she couldn't survive arrives and she has to survive it anyway. If the plot never touches the fear, the plot is decoration.

## 9. Polish is a kind of lying *(Dad's rule)*
A perfectly balanced sentence sounds like an author. A run-on that keeps going because the character can't stop thinking sounds like a *person.* Let her sentences do what a fifteen-year-old's head does: spiral, interrupt themselves, compare things to dumb specific stuff, trail off. If a line is beautiful but nobody that age would think it, it's the author showing off — cut it or rough it up. The characters build the story; the narrator just tells you where you're standing.

## 10. Every character exists for a reason — including the pet *(Dad's rule)*
Nobody gets to be in the book just because they're fun. Everyone carries a job: the coat girl is the cost of the lie, Reyes is the adult who already knows, the pet is a sentry keeping a twelve-thousand-year post, and even the warm parents exist to make the cold ones say themselves. Before you add a character, finish this sentence: *"They exist so that ___."* If the blank stays empty, they're decoration — cut them or give them a reason. (Corollary: the same goes for the world. "We are hidden" is a situation. WHY it's hidden, what the hiding cost, who's still paying — that's a story.)

---

*How to use this: pick one lesson, find it (or break it) in something we wrote, and talk about why. The goal isn't to memorize rules — it's to start* feeling *when a sentence is true and when it's faking.*
