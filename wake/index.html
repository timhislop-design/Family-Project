☄ The pitch, fast

The solar system is a healed wound. The asteroid belt isn't a failed planet. Saturn's rings aren't captured ice. Our absurdly oversized Moon isn't luck. Something passed through here once — at a scale where planets are spray — so long ago the scar looks like nature. Nobody knows. We evolved in the splash.

Strung across the outer system is a chain of crewed relay beacons, kept by families for generations, because of a law written in blood: an automated relay once let a distress call rot in its queue for nine hours. Every keeper family has its folklore — grandmother's drift readings that never resolved, the light that answers half a second early once a decade. A century of it, dismissed as lighthouse superstition.

Now automation is finally replacing the keepers. The family archives are labeled DISPOSAL — CLASS C. And a keeper's kid, doing the humiliating work of scanning the "junk," plots a hundred and forty years of superstition onto one chart.

The line comes out straight. And it points somewhere.

☄ What it means

Theme, committed: witness. Against a universe too old and too big to have ever noticed us, the unglamorous discipline of paying attention — keeping the light, writing the log no one reads — is how small lives end up mattering. Significance isn't scale. It's faithfulness. The plot enacts it: the only instrument that ever measured the truth is a century of unread, faithfully kept logs.

☄ First clay — Eli Halloran, 16

The opening of chapter one, v1, exactly as Claude committed it. This is clay. It exists to be edited hard.

The scanner spat Gran's logbook out for the third time, and Eli was pretty sure it was doing it on purpose.

DISPOSAL MANIFEST — CLASS C: SENTIMENTAL / NO EVIDENTIARY VALUE. That was the sticker the company shipped out for the family archive. An actual roll of stickers. Like the Hallorans might try to smuggle a hundred and forty years of paper past somebody.

He flattened the warped cover with his elbow and fed it through again. Every Halloran on this light had written the log every shift since before there was a company — pressure, traffic, drift, and what Gran called weather, which out here meant nothing and she recorded it anyway. The handover order said the archive should be digitized at best effort, low priority. Which was corporate for: no one will ever read this.

Fair enough. No one read it while they were writing it, either. That was sort of the whole point of a log.

Page four-hundred-and-something, Gran's handwriting going uphill the way it did when she got excited: answered 1401.997, sent 1402.5. And in the margin, her little fish-hook mark. Half a second early. A beacon can't answer half a second before you ping it, which is why nobody ever had to explain it, the same way nobody explains a creaky door.

He'd seen the fish-hook before. He flipped back. He'd seen it a lot.

The joke was supposed to be for his sister: plot every fish-hook on one chart, sixty years of them, prove Gran had been slowly losing it the way Dad always half-said she was. Ha ha. Look at the crazy scatter.

The line came out straight.

And the last point on it was three weeks old, in Dad's handwriting.

☄ Who it's for

Committed: written as grown-up science fiction that a sharp thirteen-year-old can read straight through — the band where a kid and a grandparent read the same pages and neither one is condescended to. Clean by house rules, no dumbing down. If you love The Expanse, The Martian, or Becky Chambers, this shelf is aimed at you.
